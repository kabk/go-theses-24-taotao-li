Abstract

During the 4 years in KABK, I came to the realization that my passion lies in the realm of type and moving image. The vivid imagery of Hong Kong neon signs adorned with a fusion of illustrative Chinese calligraphy and Latin words, illuminating the urban night in various captivating ways, constantly occupies my mind. I wanted to understand the hidden meanings behind color, symbols, patterns, moving methods of neon signs in Hong Kong. I wanted to know why, when neon signs are so highly valued in Hong Kong, the craft is still fading. I wanted to study why the neon signs can make a big influence in visual culture.

The neon sign in Hong Kong is one of the most iconic sceneries around the world, leaving a profound visual imprint with their dazzling and colourful presence on people.

This thesis delves into the developmental history of Hong Kong's neon signs, examining why these neon signs have evolved into a distinctive visual culture globally recognized from both visual and cultural perspectives. Additionally, the research delves into the intricate craftsmanship underlying neon sign production, the rationale governing visual synthesis, the interplay between Hong Kong's identity and neon signs, and the consequential cultural influence wielded by these neon signs.

Methodologically, the inquiry is grounded in a meticulous examination of specific urban neon signs within Hong Kong's cityscape, contextualized against pertinent policy developments, and supplemented by a synthesis of scholarly works and research literature.

Introduction

Neon signs are crafted from glass tubes filled with gas. Skilled artisans heat and manipulate the glass, creating diverse shapes that illuminate as the gas reacts to electrical energy. This soft and dynamic glow enjoyed widespread popularity in 20th-century Hong Kong. While neon lights originally hailed from the West, Hong Kong's colonial past facilitated the integration of this technology. Over several decades of evolution, Hong Kong's neon signs have transformed into a cultural hallmark. These distinctive, large neon signs, nestled within the unique urban landscape of Hong Kong, boast varied sizes, shapes, font combinations, and a spectrum of colours. Their rhythmic animated displays and the gentle radiance of neon lights collectively produce a highly identifiable visual spectacle. This iconic cityscape has found its way into numerous films, photography projects, and visual arts, becoming a symbol that captures the vibrancy, opulence, confidence, and fragility of the modern urban experience. Simultaneously, within the realm of cyberpunk sci-fi, this visual motif appears as an element in depicting a future dystopian city, offering the unique visual image – neon signs diverse interpretations and cultural significance.

As new technologies, such as LED lights, emerge with lower costs and shorter production cycles, neon signs are gradually being supplanted. Despite intentional governmental efforts to preserve them, neon signs still face an inevitable trend of decline. In the ongoing process of urban modernization, striking a balance between the imperative to protect these cultural artifacts and the developmental need of the city becomes particularly crucial.

To better achieve this goal, it is essential to understand the emotional and cultural connections between the citizens living under these neon signs and these iconic displays. From an economic perspective, exploring how the presence and evolution of neon signs impact Hong Kong's socio-economic landscape is vital. This exploration may encompass their roles in attracting tourism, shaping the distinctive characteristics of different communities, and fostering the overall economic vitality of the city.

From an urban planning standpoint, it is crucial to analyse the role of neon signs in city planning and design. Consider how neon signs contribute to the visual identity of various regions and explore existing regulations or policies governing the integration of neon signages with the cityscape. Only through a comprehensive consideration of the myriad real-world factors influencing neon signs can we hope to better protect and ensure the enduring sustainability of these beautiful neon signs.

Chapter 1: The evolution of neon signs in Hong Kong

1. The background of neon signs in Hong Kong

Neon light was discovered in 1893 by Serbian American inventor Nikola Tesla, but he didn't file for a patent. William Ramsay found that rare gases, when electrified in discharge lamps, produce distinct and vibrant colours. Later Georges Claude tweaked the design originally crafted by Daniel McFarlan Moore, ironed out some practical engineering issues, and secured a patent for the neon light in 1910. After flourishing in western nations, neon lights sailed across the seas and found their way to China. The first neon sign was installed at the Shanghai Central Hostel (上海中央大旅社) in China. It’s made by the Shanghai Far East Chemistry Company (上海远东化学制造厂) in 1927. During the decade, the neon industry in Shanghai thrived, expanding across the country, and reaching coastal cities. Yet, due to the political turmoil sparked by the Japanese invasion of China in 1937 and the ensuing internal conflicts, numerous neon light manufacturers moved their operations from Shanghai to Hong Kong. This shift involved the transfer of capital, human resources, skills, and knowledge. In the 1950s, Hong Kong's neon industry was booming, with a vibrant market showcasing intricate neon designs. Neon technology had advanced considerably progress, enabling the creation of complex shapes, resulting in brighter lights with more colors. In the 1970s, the iconic National Panasonic sign (Figure 1) became the biggest neon sign in the world. During this period, Hong Kong boasted over 80,000 neon lights, marking the pinnacle of the neon industry in the region. Sadly, because of the technology, policy, rent, building, commercial mode and so on, the presence of neon signs in Hong Kong has been steadily diminishing, since the 2000s. Nowadays the Hong Kong government and some organizations and institutions (such as M+ Museum) are trying to protect and recycle some aging and dismantled neon signs, trying to preserve Hong Kong's unique urban symbols and cultural heritage.

2. The reasons why Hong Kong's neon signs look like that

Neon signs aren't exclusive to Hong Kong, but four distinctive factors make Hong Kong's neon signs stand out. Firstly, Hong Kong's unique architectural environment plays a significant role. In terms of architectural functionality, neon signs are most found on the traditional Chinese shophouses (tong lau) and the mixed-use buildings. For them, the Ground floor is dedicated to commercial activities, while the other floors are utilized for both residential and commercial purposes. The ongoing mixed-use of buildings results in a continual increase of neon signs on different floors. Considering the density of buildings, the post-war baby boom and immigration associated with internal conflicts led to a substantial increase in the local population. As the spaces between buildings decreased and neon signs on both sides of structures continued to multiply, so it gave rise to the distinctive urban signage landscape in the city.

The second factor is the fiercely competitive business environment of that time. As noted in the book Learning from Las Vegas, commercial signs, serving a purely business function, replaced architecture and became the dominant feature of urban spaces. These signs were often unusually large, even bigger than the buildings themselves. But the lifespan of signs was notably short, akin to industrial products. This brevity wasn't due to wear and tear but rather the rapidly changing business landscape. The intense competition for space led to a constant stream of new and innovative signs. The evolution and turnover of these signs even outpaced that of the urban landscape. In Hong Kong's business scene, neon signs were crafted to be larger and in more unique shapes to attract customers in this dynamic and competitive environment.

Thirdly, the government at the time lacked specific policies to regulate these neon signs. Despite several attempts to establish standards for their appearance, permits, and installation, these efforts have often been neglected by residents. Concerning aesthetics, regulations for various signs and advertisements were already in place as early as 1912 under the Advertising Regulation Ordinance. However, due to lax enforcement, it failed to prevent people from erecting signs without proper authorization. The lack of stringent restrictions or improper policy enforcement ultimately led to excessive neon signs.

3. The reason why the neon signs in HK declined

1. Technical aspect: With the emergence of new technologies such as LED lights, which offer lower costs and shorter production cycles, neon lights are gradually being replaced.

2. Architectural aspect: Neon signs are commonly found in old districts like Hong Kong Island and Kowloon, where buildings are aging and undergoing reconstruction. Traditional Chinese shophouses (tong lau) are being dismantled, but the facades of new buildings are not designed to accommodate neon signs.

3. Commercial mode aspect: The widespread use of the internet has broadened advertising methods, reducing the need for large-scale neon signs. Furthermore, the surge in online shopping has presented a challenge to traditional brick-and-mortar retail.

4. Policy aspect: In 2010, the government implemented comprehensive regulations "Small-scale Engineering Works Control System". These regulations stipulate that signs cannot extend more than 4.2 meters from the external wall and must be at least 3.5 meters above the ground. Signs erected without approval from the Buildings Department or failing to meet these criteria are considered violations, resulting in removal orders. Iconic neon signs, like the Sammy's Kitchen cow-shaped neon sign erected since 1977 in Sai Wan, had to be taken down.

5. Rent aspect: The escalating costs of renting retail spaces in Hong Kong, combined with the uncertainty of securing the same location long-term, have discouraged businesses from investing in elaborate signage. Even if a sign is installed, the prospect of relocation involves additional expenses.

Since 2010, around 3,000 signs annually, deemed hazardous by the Buildings Department, have been mandated for removal. By now over 90% of neon signs have disappeared from the busy streets of Hong Kong. In the Yau Tsim Mong District alone, from 2015 to November 2018, a total of 803 sign owners committed to adhering to the Buildings Department's "Small-scale Engineering Works Control System" or had already completed the dismantling process.
