00: Abstract

During the four years at the Royal Academy of Arts The Hague (KABK), I came to the realization that my passion lies in the realm of type and moving image. The vivid imagery of Hong Kong neon signs adorned with a fusion of illustrative Chinese calligraphy and Latin words, illuminating the urban night in various captivating ways, constantly occupies my mind. I wished to understand the hidden meanings behind color, symbols, patterns, moving methods of neon signs in Hong Kong. I wanted to know why, when neon signs are so highly valued in Hong Kong, the craft is still fading. I wanted to study why neon signs can make an impact in visual culture. These neon signs establish iconic sceneries, leaving a profound visual imprint with their dazzling and colourful presence on people.

This thesis delves into the developmental history of Hong Kong's neon signs, examining why these neon signs have evolved into a distinctive visual culture globally recognized from both visual and cultural perspectives. Additionally, the research delves into the intricate craftsmanship underlying neon sign production, the rationale governing visual synthesis, the interplay between Hong Kong's identity and neon signs, and the consequential cultural influence wielded by these neon signs.

01: Introduction

Neon signs are crafted from glass tubes filled with gas. Skilled artisans heat and manipulate the glass, creating diverse shapes that illuminate as the gas reacts to electrical energy. This soft and dynamic glow enjoyed widespread popularity in 20th-century Hong Kong.1 While neon lights originally hailed from the West, Hong Kong's colonial past facilitated the integration of this technology.2 Over several decades of evolution, Hong Kong's neon signs have transformed into a cultural hallmark. These distinctive, large neon signs, nestled within the unique urban landscape of Hong Kong, boast varied sizes, shapes, font combinations, and a spectrum of colours. Their rhythmic animated displays and the gentle radiance of neon lights collectively produce a highly identifiable visual spectacle. This iconic cityscape has found its way into numerous films, photography projects, and visual arts, becoming a symbol that captures the vibrancy, opulence, confidence, and fragility of the modern urban experience. Simultaneously, within the realm of cyberpunk sci-fi, this visual motif appears as an element in depicting a future dystopian city, offering the unique visual image – neon signs diverse interpretations and cultural significance.

As new technologies, such as LED lights, emerge with lower costs and shorter production cycles, neon signs are gradually being supplanted. Despite intentional governmental efforts to preserve them, neon signs still face an inevitable trend of decline.3 In the ongoing process of urban modernization, striking a balance between the imperative to protect these cultural artifacts and the developmental need of the city becomes particularly crucial.

To better achieve this goal, it is essential to understand the emotional and cultural connections between the citizens living under these neon signs and these iconic displays. From an economic perspective, exploring how the presence and evolution of neon signs impact Hong Kong's socio-economic landscape is vital. This exploration may encompass their roles in attracting tourism, shaping the distinctive characteristics of different communities, and fostering the overall economic vitality of the city.

From an urban planning standpoint, it is crucial to analyse the role of neon signs in city planning and design. Consider how neon signs contribute to the visual identity of various regions and explore existing regulations or policies governing the integration of neon signages with the cityscape. Only through a comprehensive consideration of the myriad real-world factors influencing neon signs can we hope to better protect and ensure the enduring sustainability of these beautiful neon signs.

02: The evolution of neon signs in Hong Kong

    1. The background of neon signs in Hong Kong

Neon light was discovered in 1893 by Serbian American inventor Nikola Tesla, but he didn't file for a patent. William Ramsay found that rare gases, when electrified in discharge lamps, produce distinct and vibrant colours. Later Georges Claude tweaked the design originally crafted by Daniel McFarlan Moore, ironed out some practical engineering issues, and secured a patent for the neon light in 1910.4 After flourishing in western nations, neon lights sailed across the seas and found their way to China. The first neon sign was installed at the Shanghai Central Hostel (上海中央大旅社) in China. It’s made by the Shanghai Far East Chemistry Company (上海远东化学制造厂) in 1927.5 During the decade, the neon industry in Shanghai thrived, expanding across the country, and reaching coastal cities. Yet, due to the political turmoil sparked by the Japanese invasion of China in 1937 and the ensuing internal conflicts, numerous neon light manufacturers moved their operations from Shanghai to Hong Kong.6 This shift involved the transfer of capital, human resources, skills, and knowledge. In the 1950s, Hong Kong's neon industry was booming, with a vibrant market showcasing intricate neon designs. Neon technology had advanced considerably progress, enabling the creation of complex shapes, resulting in brighter lights with more colors. In the 1970s, the iconic National Panasonic sign (Figure 1) became the biggest neon sign in the world.7 During this period, Hong Kong boasted over 80,000 neon lights, marking the pinnacle of the neon industry in the region. Sadly, because of the technology, policy, rent, building, commercial mode and so on, the presence of neon signs in Hong Kong has been steadily diminishing, since the 2000s. Nowadays the Hong Kong government and some organizations and institutions (such as M+ Museum) are trying to protect and recycle some aging and dismantled neon signs, trying to preserve Hong Kong's unique urban symbols and cultural heritage.8

    2. The reasons why Hong Kong's neon signs look like that

Neon signs aren't exclusive to Hong Kong, but four distinctive factors make Hong Kong's neon signs stand out. Firstly, Hong Kong's unique architectural environment plays a significant role. In terms of architectural functionality, neon signs are most found on the traditional Chinese shophouses (tong lau) and the mixed-use buildings. For them, the Ground floor is dedicated to commercial activities, while the other floors are utilized for both residential and commercial purposes. The ongoing mixed-use of buildings results in a continual increase of neon signs on different floors. Considering the density of buildings, the post-war baby boom and immigration associated with internal conflicts led to a substantial increase in the local population. As the spaces between buildings decreased and neon signs on both sides of structures continued to multiply, so it gave rise to the distinctive urban signage landscape in the city.9

The second factor is the fiercely competitive business environment of that time. As noted in the book Learning from Las Vegas,10 commercial signs, serving a purely business function, replaced architecture and became the dominant feature of urban spaces. These signs were often unusually large, even bigger than the buildings themselves. But the lifespan of signs was notably short, akin to industrial products. This brevity wasn't due to wear and tear but rather the rapidly changing business landscape. The intense competition for space led to a constant stream of new and innovative signs. The evolution and turnover of these signs even outpaced that of the urban landscape.11 In Hong Kong's business scene, neon signs were crafted to be larger and in more unique shapes to attract customers in this dynamic and competitive environment.

Thirdly, the government at the time lacked specific policies to regulate these neon signs. Despite several attempts to establish standards for their appearance, permits, and installation, these efforts have often been neglected by residents. Concerning aesthetics, regulations for various signs and advertisements were already in place as early as 1912 under the Advertising Regulation Ordinance.12 However, due to lax enforcement, it failed to prevent people from erecting signs without proper authorization. The lack of stringent restrictions or improper policy enforcement ultimately led to excessive neon signs.13

    3. The reasons why the neon signs in Hongkong declined

There are several reasons why the neon signs in Hongkong declined. Firstly, the emergence of new technologies, such as LED lights, which offer lower costs and shorter production cycles, is gradually replacing neon lights. Secondly, neon signs, once ubiquitous in older districts like Hong Kong Island and Kowloon, are facing challenges as traditional buildings are dismantled for reconstruction, with new facades often not designed to accommodate them. Moreover, the widespread use of the internet has broadened advertising methods, reducing the necessity for large-scale neon signs, while the surge in online shopping poses a challenge to traditional brick-and-mortar retail. In addition, policy-wise, the government's implementation of regulations like the "Small-scale Engineering Works Control System" in 2010 imposes strict criteria for sign placement, leading to the removal of many iconic neon signs for violations.14 Finally, escalating costs of renting retail spaces in Hong Kong, coupled with uncertainties in securing long-term locations, have discouraged businesses from investing in elaborate neon signs, even if installed, relocation often entails additional expenses.

Since 2010, around 3,000 signs annually, deemed hazardous by the Buildings Department, have been mandated for removal. By now over 90% of neon signs have disappeared from the busy streets of Hong Kong.15 In the Yau Tsim Mong District alone, from 2015 to November 2018, a total of 803 sign owners committed to adhering to the Buildings Department's "Small-scale Engineering Works Control System" or had already completed the dismantling process. 

03: The visual secrets behind neon signs in Hong Kong

    1. The decorative frames of neon signs

Nam Wah Neonlight & Electrical Factory16 donated a collection of 218 original neon sign designs to the the Information Design Lab at Hong Kong Polytechnic University.17 They traced the outlines of each of the 175 sign designs and observed their transformation over time to analyse the form and frame of the neon signs. In the study of horizontal neon signs, there were more irregular arcs and graphic shapes with less single shapes and combined shapes in the 1950s (Figure 2), half regular form and half curved shapes in the 1960s (Figure 3) and signs became simple and clean in the 1970s (Figure 4). As we can see, the aesthetic about frames of neon signs transferred from decorative style to more simple and functional style.18

    2. The colour of neon signs in Hong Kong

The magic behind the soft and bright colours of neon signs is long luminous gas-discharge tubes that contain rarefied neon or other gases.19 By infusing gases like neon, helium, argon, krypton, or xenon and mixing them with a fluorescent coating (called a "phosphor") of various colours inside glass tubes, we can create a range of colours including red, yellow, blue, white, green, and purple.20 As the technology developed, more precise colours are created. In the design of neon signs, the most common colour combinations are complementary, such as red and green, red and blue, or yellow and blue. Occasionally, designers (Hu Zhikai, Nam Wah Neonlight & Electrical Factory) may introduce one or more intermediate colours to the combinations. Among all these colours, red stands out as the most used, particularly for headline text.

    3. The stroke of neon signs

The outlining technique in neon signs is usually divided into single-stroke and outline (Figure 5). In the single-stroke method, the lines follow the structures of the text, while the outline traces the outer edges of the characters. Sometimes, to highlight the titles in a sign, they will add a single-stroke based on the outline (single inline) or repeating the outline (multiple inline). In some instances, they fill the outline with horizontally arranged light tubes to achieve uniform illumination (parallel lines). However, this typically involves more complex craftsmanship and comes with higher costs.21

In Hong Kong, double-stroke is commonly used for titles, whereas single-stroke is more often applied to subtitles and additional information. The outlining method used for patterns and border decorations is more flexible, intended to highlight and emphasize their shapes.

    4. Graphic symbols in neon signs

As the business environment becomes increasingly competitive, regular text and slogans are no longer sufficient to meet the demands of market promotion. Hence, more signs with graphic symbols have emerged. The graphic symbols in neon signs typically consist of simple lines and shapes, as overly complex images might risk breakage due to the excessive twisting of the glass tubes. These graphic symbols can be broadly categorized into five types: Animal/Botanical, Characters, Objects, Buildings/Scenes, and Geometric forms.

Typically, the selection of patterns is driven by the shop's function or the symbolism the shop wishes to convey. For instance, a store specializing in roast duck or roast chicken might straightforwardly feature graphic symbols of ducks and chickens. In certain Chinese seafood restaurants, you'll often find the use of double carp symbols, as carp is seen as the embodiment of a dragon in Chinese mythology. The Chinese word for "fish" sounds like "surplus," carrying special auspicious significance for business owners and believed to attract good fortune.22

    5. The typefaces of neon signs (Chinese and English)

The distinctive charm of Hong Kong's neon signs owes much to the variety of fonts employed, especially notable in the sign of the city's older districts. These fonts span a broad spectrum, including styles like clerical script, Northern Wei regular script, Bang script, bold regular script, and more, with a notable prevalence of the Northern Wei regular script and regular script.

In many storefront signs and plaques, handwritten calligraphy is often executed by Au Kin Kung, a skilled calligrapher adept in various styles, with a notable proficiency in Northern Wei regular script. As the Northern Wei regular script exhibits a strong stylistic presence, slightly modified and innovated strokes are applied by Au Kin Kung to enhance clarity and better suit distant recognition when shaping the outlines and frames of the characters.23

In contrast to the diverse Chinese font styles seen in various signs, there are few choices for western fonts, often utilizing sans-serif uppercase fonts, cursive, and Roman serif fonts. Western scripts are commonly used as subtitles in signs, presenting a distinct contrast to the multitudinous Chinese font styles.24

    6. The composition of information on neon signs

An important factor influencing sign composition is the method of suspension, typically categorized as projecting vertical signs or projecting horizontal signs25. In the case of projecting vertical signs, text information follows the traditional top-to-bottom Chinese format. The store's name takes center stage, often occupying the largest portion, while complementary details find placement at the top, middle, or bottom of the sign. This additional information could encompass business particulars, featured dishes in a restaurant, specialized services, or the store's English name, usually presented in a smaller font. Details positioned at the bottom are typically geared towards pedestrians, whereas those at the top or middle sections are strategically placed for optimal visibility by drivers from a distance26.

For projecting horizontal signs, the reading logic is from left to right. The store's name commands the majority of the sign, and supplementary information is typically positioned at the top or bottom, occasionally on the left side for better visibility by pedestrians.

04: The visual culture of neon signs in Hong Kong

    1. The aesthetic of cyberpunk

The term "cyberpunk" originated from the science fiction literary movement that emerged in the 1960s and 1970s, culminating in the 1980s. Coined by science fiction writer Bruce Bethke27 in 1980, it was popularized by William Gibson's novel "Neuromancer"28 in 1984, establishing a new genre within science fiction literature. With its diverse visual impact, including neon lights on streets, iconic advertisements and towering skyscrapers introduced a captivating visual aesthetic to the genre.

Cyberpunk literature is imbued with a strong sense of anti-utopianism29 and pessimism, portraying a future society where the gap between the rich and the poor widens incessantly, and where the entire social fabric is saturated with oppression of human nature.30 In Hong Kong's history, the Kowloon Walled City stands out with its staggering residential density, aimless urban development, and proliferation of illicit businesses. There are so many similarities between the visual image of the Kowloon Walled City and the world depicted in cyberpunk literature, thus it becomes quintessential archetype of urban architecture within cyberpunk narratives.31

In addition to novels depicting the cyber world, films and television series have more directly imprinted this visual imagery into the public consciousness, for instance, the 1982 film "Blade Runner"32, the 2017 sequel "Blade Runner 2049"33, the 2008 film "Babylon A.D."34 and so on.

The 1995 animated film "Ghost in the Shell"35 deepened the association between Hong Kong and cyberpunk. Director Mamoru Oshii envisioned Asia as the vanguard of the future world's development in the 21st century, with Hong Kong serving as a template for emulation by other cities of the time. Thus, he selected Hong Kong as the blueprint for the futuristic metropolis "New Port City" depicted in the movie.36

After numerous visits to observe Hong Kong's streets and alleys and sketching drafts, Mamoru Oshii meticulously crafted the details of "New Port City" portrayed in the film. The result was a portrayal so rich in detail that one could almost instantly recognize Hong Kong. The sight of massive planes flying low over densely populated residential buildings was inspired by the daily scenes near the former Kai Tak Airport in Kowloon City37. Iconic Hong Kong street trams were depicted as yachts in the animation, while the narrow streets transformed into pitch-black waterways, creating a surreal yet hauntingly authentic depiction of a dystopian urban landscape.38

The 2017 live-action adaptation of "Ghost in the Shell"39 remained true to Mamoru Oshii's vision by shooting on location in Hong Kong. The film showcased recognizable landmarks such as the circular footbridge in Causeway Bay, the lively streets of Yau Ma Tei, and the towering skyscrapers along both sides of Victoria Harbour. With the addition of CGI effects, Hong Kong was heightened into a mesmerizing spectacle, solidifying its status as a quintessential reference point in Western cyberpunk works. Neon signs, in particular, continued to serve as iconic elements of cyberpunk visual aesthetics in the film.40

    2. The neon signs’ cultural significance to citizens in Hong Kong

The older generation of Hong Kong residents, who have witnessed the evolution of neon lights from the 1950s to the present, hold a deep emotional connection to these luminous symbols. For them, neon signs are not just a cultural symbol of Hong Kong but also a nostalgic memory of their childhood that they can never return to. However, since the 2000s, due to the aging of many neon signs, policies from the Buildings Department mandated the removal of many still-operational neon signs.41 This was because aging signs posed risks of loosening and falling, creating safety hazards. The removed aging neon signs are often sent to conservation organizations like the M+ Museum42, where they are transformed into museum artifacts for historical research and public appreciation. However, as they transition from street displays to museum exhibits, these once-functional neon signs now remain perpetually dark. The vibrant and colorful images of neon signs that once illuminated the streets on those memorable nights have vanished from people's sights.43

For Xie Zhenyuan, the six-meter-high neon sign of "Tai Tung Bakery" once symbolized the long history of this old brand. His shop endured the tumultuous years when Hong Kong was under Japanese occupation during World War II. At his age of 90, Xie Zhenyuan witnessed the removal of the neon sign in his store in 2022. He was informed that the sign was too large and aging, failing to meet regulations. "That sign has been hanging for over 50 years, weathered typhoons, and never stop working," he remarked. He continues to visit the shop daily. He missed the neon sign. To comply with the government's demands, Xie Zhenyuan plans to install a smaller sign for the bakery, despite the hefty cost of HK$620,000. To ensure the business is passed down to the fourth generation, his son has returned from Australia to Hong Kong. "I hope Hong Kong remains vibrant," expressed Xie Zhenyuan. "I want this shop to embody the essence of Hong Kong for people."44

From this interview, we discern the profound attachment of the older generation to Hong Kong's neon signs. Meanwhile, the younger generation of Hong Kongers, influenced by early films, the few remaining neon signs on the streets, and the impact of their parents, perceive a condensed history of neon signs.45 Due to its colonial past under British rule and its current status under Chinese governance, Hong Kong is in a complex cultural context. Hong Kongers are eagerly seeking their own special identity, distinct from that of Britain or mainland China, amidst this backdrop.46 Therefore, neon signs, as cultural vessels, offer a sense of self-identification and harbor nostalgic sentiments for many Hong Kongers. Despite various challenges leading to the decline of neon signs, most Hong Kongers are keen to preserve and uphold this cultural identity.47

    3. Sustainable development of neon signs, policy management, and technology iteration

When faced with unchangeable policies, the most effective means of preserving these cultural treasures is through conservation (Bao Yu).48 Many conservation projects have been improving over time. Conservationists not only meticulously document the visual and textual aspects but also analyze and deconstruct the history of each neon sign, which may include analyzing fonts (many of them are handwritten by calligraphy masters, making each sign unique), color schemes, and materials. They then had these analyses and studies, make them open for people to study and research.49

The Information Design Lab at the department of design, Hong Kong Polytechnic University, has collected numerous neon sign sketches dating from the 1950s to the 1970s. Utilizing special printing processes, they have compiled image collections of neon signs showing different effects during the day and night. Readers can utilize the UV light included with the books to observe the neon signs at night. Additionally, they have published a variety of neon sign collection books.50

In addition to conservation and various cultural product displays, there has been a development of neon signs in the world of electronic gaming, which is currently in its golden age. The scale of the gaming industry is immense, surpassing even the combined popularity of mainstream film and music industries. It is currently one of the hottest forms of popular media. There are approximately 150 electronic games set in Hong Kong globally (Figure 6). Hugh Davies explores the portrayal of Hong Kong in gaming. Many scenes that have disappeared from the real world of Hong Kong are revived in the virtual gaming world, such as Kowloon Walled City and some dismantled neon signs. In the virtual world, Hong Kong and neon signs exist in another form in people's minds.51

The government organizes various activities related to neon signs, such as inviting different artists to create neon-themed works, organizing neon-themed exhibitions, and creating neon sign maps, all aimed at maintaining the cultural influence of neon signs in the hearts of citizens. In recent years, many local companies, as well as numerous independent restaurants, bars, and fashion brands, have been committed to providing a new home for neon signs, transitioning them from the streets to indoor spaces and transforming them into genuine works of decorative art.52

Conclusion

Neon signs were introduced to Hong Kong from the West, and due to Hong Kong's unique geographical location and architectural features, they gave rise to a one-of-a-kind neon landscape in the world. Flourishing amidst the fiercely competitive business environment of the time and the absence of policy constraints, neon lights grew unrestrained. This process fostered a generation of skilled artisans to meet the high demand. Through continuous iterations and developments in both technology and aesthetics, more beautiful and unique neon signs were created. These neon signs accompanied a generation of people growing up from the 1950s to the 2000s, becoming cultural symbols in their hearts and nostalgic memories. Concurrently, due to the vibrant development of Hong Kong cinema during this period, neon signs became a special scene and symbol in visual culture. Additionally, due to the portrayal in dystopian science fiction novels, the imagery of Hong Kong's neon signs represented an imagination of the future world, with cyberpunk becoming inseparably linked to Hong Kong's neon signs. During this period, Hong Kong's neon signs acquired more cultural definitions.

However, after 2000, due to the iteration of new technologies and the safety risks posed by aging neon signs, tens of thousands of neon lights were forcibly removed and became exhibits in museums. In this period, Hong Kong citizens, who were immersed in a blend of Eastern and Western cultures, urgently needed to find symbols and voices that belonged to their own city. Thus, neon lights became a symbol for Hong Kong people to find their identity and belonging. Despite the irreversible trend of decline in neon lights, Hong Kong citizens continue to strive to protect their cultural identity symbol—the neon lights. Through conservation efforts, research and analysis, organizing neon light-related activities, producing peripheral cultural products, and recreating scenes in numerous electronic games, the neon lights of Hong Kong continue to shine brightly in people's hearts, never to be extinguished.
